## This Week's Verdict

One Oracle PeopleSoft zero-day, an ever-growing list of victims, and the dawning realization that "we only lost the boring data" is now an official PR genre. Meanwhile, the people charged with poisoning your tap water are getting in through passwords your grandmother would be embarrassed to use. Same week, different decade — it's all already happened before.

## The Breaches

The story this week is not a breach. It's a *campaign*, and it has a name: ShinyHunters, working their way through Oracle PeopleSoft deployments via a zero-day. **Nissan** is the marquee victim, disclosing a breach of current and former employee data tied directly to the same exploitation chain. Employee records — the gift that keeps on giving to whoever runs the next round of social engineering.

Then there's the **National Association of Insurance Commissioners**, breached through the same PeopleSoft hole, who would very much like you to know they only lost "publicly available data, outdated logs, and configuration files." Take a breath before you accept that framing. Config files and logs are exactly what an attacker wants when they're mapping the *next* environment — internal hostnames, service accounts, integration paths. "Only outdated logs" is the breach-disclosure equivalent of "it's just a flesh wound."

What both incidents reveal is the real lesson: a single vulnerable enterprise platform, sitting in the supply chain of thousands of organizations, turns one bug into a buffet. ShinyHunters didn't have to be clever a hundred times. They had to be clever once.

## Vulnerabilities Worth Your Attention

The Oracle **PeopleSoft zero-day** behind the ShinyHunters spree is the only vulnerability that earns top billing this week, and it earns it on body count alone. The reporting frames it as a zero-day exploited in active data-theft attacks, which means the window where you got to patch ahead of the adversary already closed. If you run PeopleSoft — HR, financials, campus solutions, any of it — you are not a bystander to the Nissan and NAIC stories. You are a potential sequel.

Treat any internet-reachable PeopleSoft instance as suspect until proven otherwise. Apply Oracle's fixes the moment they're available if you haven't already, and assume that "patched" and "clean" are two different questions. The second one requires you to go look.

## Threat Actors & Campaigns

**ShinyHunters** continue to do what extortion-focused data thieves do best: find one scalable weakness and industrialize it. This isn't ransomware theater with skulls on the screen — it's quiet theft and the threat of publication. Less drama, same leverage.

On the nation-state side, **Iran, Russia, and China** are all reportedly probing water systems for sabotage potential. The uncomfortable detail buried in that headline is *how*: weak passwords, internet-exposed PLCs, and segmentation that exists only in the architecture diagram. These are not zero-days. These are 1999-days. The attackers don't need a research budget when the front door is propped open with a brick that says `admin/admin`.

And in the category of "things that quietly hoover up your life," Microsoft caught a **malicious Chrome extension impersonating Perplexity**, the AI search engine. It logged searches and intercepted everything typed into the address bar — every query, every keystroke, every half-typed URL you reconsidered — routing it through an attacker server before passing you along to the real results. Google pulled it after disclosure. How many people installed an "AI search assistant" without a second thought is left as an exercise for the reader.

## The Bigger Picture

Two threads run through this week, and they tie into the same knot.

The first is **concentration risk**. Oracle PeopleSoft is not exotic — it's plumbing. When attackers find a flaw in the plumbing, every building connected to it floods at once. We keep treating breaches as individual failures of individual companies, but Nissan and NAIC didn't fail independently. They shared an exposure. The modern attack surface isn't your network; it's everyone's network running the same software you do. Your security posture is increasingly a function of vendor decisions you had no part in making.

The second thread is the **persistent fantasy that threats are sophisticated**. The water-system reporting should be required reading for anyone who buys "next-gen AI-powered" defense tooling to stop attacks that, in reality, walk in through default credentials. Nation-states targeting critical infrastructure aren't burning their best exploits on your pump controller. They don't have to. The gap between the threats we fear and the threats that actually work remains enormous, and we keep spending on the wrong side of it.

The malicious Perplexity extension is the consumer-grade version of the same disease: a shiny AI label disarms the part of your brain that would otherwise ask "why does my search bar need a third-party listener?" The attackers understand that the word "AI" now functions as a sedative. Plan accordingly.

## Patch. Now.

A short list, in order of how much it should be ruining your week:

- **Patch Oracle PeopleSoft immediately** and hunt for compromise — this zero-day is being actively exploited by ShinyHunters. Pull internet-facing instances behind access controls and review logs for unexpected data access. "Patched" is not "clean."
- **Audit your PeopleSoft data exposure.** If you got hit, employee PII and config/log data are the likely losses. Start notification and credential-rotation planning now, not after the extortion email.
- **Go find your exposed OT.** Inventory internet-reachable PLCs and HMIs, kill default and weak credentials, and verify your network segmentation actually exists outside the diagram. This is the water-system attack path, and it's trivially fixable.
